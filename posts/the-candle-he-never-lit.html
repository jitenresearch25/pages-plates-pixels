The Candle He Never Lit

A Diwali story about poverty, kindness, and one sentence that stayed forever.

On Diwali night, every house in the village looked bright. Candles stood near doors. Diyas glowed on windows. Children laughed under strings of light.

But in one small house, there was only darkness. There was no electricity, no money for decorations, not even enough to buy one small candle. Still, a little boy had only one wish: “Just one candle. Then our home will also look like Diwali.”

He knew that after candles burned out, a little wax always remained near the doorsteps. So while others celebrated, he walked quietly from house to house and collected tiny leftover pieces of wax. A little from one door. A little from another. Slowly, his hands filled with broken pieces of someone else’s Diwali.

On the way back, he saw one last candle burning outside a house. He waited silently near the door. Suddenly, the owner opened the door and shouted, “You thief! If you cannot even buy a candle, then don’t dream of celebrating Diwali.”

The boy froze. He opened his small hands and looked at all the wax he had collected — pieces of hope, pieces of light, pieces of a festival he only wanted to feel once. Then he threw them away.

Years passed. The boy grew up. Now he could buy not one, but a thousand candles. But every Diwali, his house remained dark. Not because he could not afford light anymore, but because one cruel sentence had blown out something inside him that no candle could ever relight.

Sometimes poverty does not hurt as much as the way people make you feel poor. Be kind. Your words may become someone’s memory forever.
